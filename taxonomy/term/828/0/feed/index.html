Industrial Workers of the World - Wisconsin General Strike https://www.iww.org/taxonomy/term/828/0 en Wisconsin's New Free Speech Restrictions https://www.iww.org/content/wisconsins-new-free-speech-restrictions <p><img align="right" src="http://www.iww.org/sites/default/files/images/GSDrookEngsm.preview.jpg" alt="" />Since the February uprising in Wisconsin, which began with a three-week occupation of the Capitol in Madison, the building has been home to a variety of demonstrations and political actions.&nbsp; What perhaps stands out the most is the Solidarity Sing-along, which draws upwards of 100 participants and has been going on for more than 40 weeks.&nbsp; The singers gather in the rotunda each weekday at noon to sing songs that include both traditional labor and protest songs and some new songs penned in the months since Governor Scott Walker first introduced his plans to bust the public employee unions and impose devastating austerity measures on the working class of Wisconsin.</p> <p>In recent months police have begun arresting and ticketing people for trivial &quot;violations&quot;, such as wearing hats or sunglasses in the Senate and Assembly galleries, holding signs, or exercising their right (protected by the Wisconsin constitution) to record legislative sessions.&nbsp; These restrictions have apparently been a lead-up to the real crackdown.&nbsp; Just last week, the Walker administration announced a new policy that will severely curtail the free speech rights exercised by the Solidarity Sing-along and other groups who use the Capitol building.&nbsp;</p> <p>The new rules require groups of four or more people to apply for a permit from the DOA at least 72 hours in advance &ldquo;for all activity and displays in state buildings&rdquo;.&nbsp; Groups can be charged $50 per hour per police officer if law enforcement is determined to be necessary.&nbsp; Payment for law enforcement could be required in advance as part of the permit process and protesters could face additional charges for liability insurance.&nbsp; The rules are vague and arbitrary, and so far the administration has refused to clarify them or answer questions from citizens about how they will be enforced, leaving room for abuse by police and DOA officials.</p> <p>These rules are obviously aimed at making free speech inconvenient and restrictively expensive for most people.&nbsp; Free speech is a hard-won right for Americans.&nbsp; Between 1907 and 1916, free speech demonstrations lead by the IWW swept the western United States.&nbsp; In Spokane, the <em>Industrial Worker</em> published a call to all workers to defend their rights: &quot;Wanted -- Men to Fill the Jails&rdquo;.&nbsp; And fill the jails they did!&nbsp; As one Wobbly got dragged from the soapbox and arrested, another would take his place and the call would go out for more &ldquo;footloose rebels&rdquo; from all over the country to hop on a train and come join the fight.&nbsp; There was a near demobilization, as the collective bargaining law was hung up in the courts and the demonstrations attracted fewer and fewer people. Walker says the bill is estimated to save $30 million to help pay down a $137 million budget deficit, but the cuts are being taken directly out of the public sector. On Feb. 17, the situation escalated as 14 senate Democrats fled to Illinois to block passage of the bill. In order to pass any fiscal-related measure, 20 senators are needed to make quorum, and the remaining eight Republicans could not fit the bill.
